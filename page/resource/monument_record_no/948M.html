description The Playhouse or Jacob's Wells Theatre located on the west side of Woodwells Lane (modern Jacobs Wells Road). and lying just outside the boundary of the city. The theatre was purpose-built for its first owner, the actor John Hippisley, on a site immediately outside the Bristol county boundary. The building had a 12.5 metre wide frontage on Jacob's Wells Road and extended backwards to the south-west. The form of the building is currently unclear but it was rectangular, measuring 42 metres from north-east to south-west, and narrowed to 8.5 metres in width to the rear of the element on the street front. The theatre opened on 3 July 1729 (Barker 1974, 4) and aimed to draw much of its audience from those visiting the spa at Hotwells. It staged a summer season of plays performed by actors established on the London stage.
Hippisley still owned the theatre at his death in c.1748 and it was inherited by his wife who appears to have sold the lease to a consortium but continued to act as Box-Book Keeper (Barker 1974, 5). The seasons included some performances with a strong political resonance in the Bristol context; notably Southerne's stage adaptation of Oroonoko which was staged three times in the summer of 1763. The fortunes of the theatre declined as the spa waned in importance and competition emerged with the opening of the New Theatre in 1766 (BUAD 15M; later renamed Theatre Royal). The leading actor of one of the final seasons,.William Powell, was invited to lead the New Theatre company and the Jacob's Wells Theatre appears have closed after the 1765 season.
The playhouse was subsequently demolished and replaced by cottages, which were in turn removed for "industrial housing," and is now the site of post-Second World War flats.
