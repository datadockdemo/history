description The Royal Fort, located at the summit of St. Michael's Hill. A fort, known as Windmill Hill fort, was built on the site in 1643 and after the fall of Bristol to the Royalists in that year it was substantially reworked. A Royal order for the work was issued in the summer of 1644 (Cal. S. P. Dom. 1644-1645 235) and it was apparently completed in December of that year. Renamed the Royal Fort, the fort was the most powerful of the city's defensive fortifications during the English Civil Wars (1642-1648).
There has been some speculation about the exact footprint of the fort but geophysical survey by the University of Southampton in 1999 (BUAD 3479) together with documentary research by Dr. Roger Leech (Leech 2000, 20-21) have suggested a more precise position. The fort occupied the area defined by Royal Fort Road, University Walk, St. Michael's Hill and Tyndall Avenue. It was pentangular in form and the surrounding ditch was approximately 5 metres wide. Both the fortifications and the internal buildings appear to have been of Brandon Hill grit. Little is currently known about the internal organisation of the fort, although a gatehouse (BUAD 131M) in the eastern wall survives at the western end of Royal Fort Road.
The Royal Fort played a major part in the siege and storming of Bristol by the New Model Army in 1645.
The site was developed for housing from 1655 (Bettey 1997, 7) and the process of the removal of the fabric of the fort appears to have been gradual. Elements were still extant in the mid-eighteenth century and many of these were recorded in drawings by James Stewart in the 1750s. The Ordnance Survey first edition plan surveyed in 1883 clearly shows part of the north bastion in the garden of Cromwell House. Currently the gatehouse and a length of walling survive.
The Royal Fort gatehouse has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/10/237).
