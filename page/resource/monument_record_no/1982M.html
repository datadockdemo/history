description Anti-aircraft battery, Purdown. The site was a heavy anti-aircraft battery located on the crest of the south-west to north-east ridge of Purdown. The site was first occupied on November 11th 1939 by mobile anti-aircraft guns for the anti-aircraft defence of Bristol. In June 1940, it was converted into a permanent four-gun position with two more positions being added later in the war. During the Second World War Bristol was defended by about twenty anti-aircraft batteries. Purdown was one of eight which were fixed.
The battery consisted originally of four octagonal gun positions on the south-western side of the battery, each with three ammunition bays. At the centre of the position was a complex containing the command post and associated buildings, while the two magazines were sited on the north-west side of the site. Dobinson indicates that the battery armament of four 3.7in static guns remained the same between the summer of 1941 and January 1943 and that the battery did not have radar (Dobinson 1996, 330). Two additional square gun positions built of reinforced concrete blocks were added later (Roberts 1981). Early in the war the battery was also equipped with Lewis gun emplacements. An attack on the 2 December 1940 resulted in a high explosive bomb wounding of the crew of one of these emplacements, one member later dying of wounds (Penny 1997, 15-16). Immediately to the south-west of the battery was Purdown Camp (SMR 21027), ancillary buildings probably including sleeping quarters, canteen, sickbay etc.
The battery is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM No. AV 184). There are photographs of the monument in the SMR collection of Ground-based Photographs, numbers 1604-1609 inclusive.
