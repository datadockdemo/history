description Lower Arcade, a covered shopping arcade located on the northern side of Broadmead. The arcade, one of the earliest in the country, was constructed in 1824 to a design by James Foster jun. (Ison, 1952 34) and runs from south-east to north-west between Broadmead and The Horsefair. It was the southern half of a long arcade divided by The Horsefair, the northern half, the Upper Arcade (BUAD 616M), being destroyed by bombing during the Second World War.
The design clearly drew on that of the Burlington Arcade in London of 1818 (Morrison, 2003 101) and was in a Greek Revival style. The external, south-facing, elevation on Broadmead front has a Pennant sandstone ashlar plinth supporting a freestone ashlar fa�ade and fluted Ionic columns on either side of the entrance. On either side of these are plate-glass shopfronts flanked by pilasters. Above is an entablature bearing the legend "THE ARCADE" with a frieze of palmettes above and a tall parapet.
Internally the arcade consists of a central footway 82 metres long with shops on its eastern and western sides. Large two-storey Ionic pilasters between pairs of shops support architraves on either of the arcade. Above is a frieze containing decorative iron grilles for a ventilation system. The cornice over the frieze supports wide, triangular-section cast-iron cross-members, panelled on the lower side and with moulded lion's heads on the faces. Between these large skylights provide the light for the central walkway. The individual shops are single-bay units of three storeys and attic with a basement and are constructed of brick bonded in a grey-white lime mortar with charcoal inclusions. They originally appear to have conformed to a similar plan, each pair of shops having a mirrored plan. The ground floor originally consisted of a single-bay shop with a recessed entrance doorway on the northern or southern side. In the northern or southern corner at the rear of the shop there was a spiral stair which served all floors of the building. A partition wall separated off a small rectangular storeroom from the main area of the shop. The ground-floor storeroom and the landings for the stair on the upper floors were accommodated in a projecting rear block. At first- and second-floor the stair opened on to a small landing with a short rail, the balusters having a plain, square cross-section. This gave on to a single main room with a chimneybreast on either the northern or southern wall. The attic storey consisted of a single L-shaped space with the stair and landing enclosed by a partition wall.
The roof on each row of shops is a mansard with projecting eight-flue stacks on the line of the party wall between each pair of shops. The roof was recovered with concrete tiles in the late-twentieth century.
An arched alley, accessed by a door from the cellar of each shop, runs the full length of the arcade beneath the central footway.
The arcade has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/40/479).
